# Is there any published data on the revenue impact of online directory listings for healthcare providers? Specifically:

- Studies measuring patient volume increases from directory listings (Healthgrades, Zocdoc, Psychology Today, specialty directories)
- ROI studies on healthcare directory advertising vs other marketing channels
- Any data showing how many patients per month/year a typical directory listing generates for therapy practices
- Comparison of cost-per-acquisition between directory listings, Google Ads, social media, and physician referrals for therapy practices

This would help me quantify the actual expected patient volume from a directory listing, rather than relying on theoretical LTV calculations. Peer-reviewed or industry survey data preferred.

Short answer: there is **almost no rigorous, quantified research** that will let you say, “A listing on X directory produces Y new patients per month and Z ROI” for therapy practices. What exists is mostly:

- Patient surveys about **online reviews and rating sites generally**, not specific directories.
- Vendor or consultancy content with **claims but no transparent methodology**.
- A few industry surveys about **how important directories are as a referral source**, but not patient‑volume or CAC benchmarks.

Below is what can be supported with real data, by your bullets.

***

## 1. Studies measuring patient‑volume increases from directory listings

There are **no peer‑reviewed studies** that cleanly measure pre/post patient volume attributable to being listed on Healthgrades, Zocdoc, Psychology Today, etc., especially not for PT/OT/SLP/ABA practices.

What exists is:

### a) Evidence that patients heavily use online review/directory sites

- A 2012 JAMA survey of >2,100 U.S. adults found **59%** considered physician‑rating websites “somewhat important” or “very important” when choosing a physician.[^1]
- A later survey (>2,400 patients) cited in an orthopedics commentary found **82% of patients use online reviews when evaluating physicians**, and **72% use them as their first step** when searching for a new doctor.[^1]
- A 2022 Reputation/YouGov survey (n≈1,000 U.S. consumers) reported **72% of consumers read online ratings and reviews when considering booking with a new healthcare provider**, and 72% prefer providers rated 4/5 stars or higher.[^2]

These show that **online rating/directory sites matter a lot to patients**, but they do **not** quantify:

- “Patients per month per listing,” or
- Incremental volume after adding a listing.


### b) Healthgrades engagement uplift (not patient counts)

Healthgrades, in B2B content, reports that:

- Physician profiles with a **3‑star rating or higher see a 150% increase in engagement** versus lower‑rated profiles.[^3]

“Engagement” here is clicks, views, or interactions on Healthgrades, not booked visits. No conversion to actual patient volume is published.

### c) Psychology Today therapist directories – usage, not yield

Psychology Today marketing copy says:

- “More therapy‑seekers come to Psychology Today in their search for the perfect therapist than to any other mental health resource” and
- The directory has **“millions of therapy seekers”** and is the “\#1 source of online referrals.”[^4]

A Wellnite article cites platform stats that **Psychology Today’s directory sees nearly 1 million unique U.S. visitors per week**.[^5]

But again, there are **no audited numbers tying those visits to average new clients per provider**.

***

## 2. ROI studies: directory advertising vs other channels

There are **no peer‑reviewed ROI comparisons** that pit Healthgrades/Zocdoc/Psychology Today against Google Ads, social media, or physician referrals in terms of CAC for therapy practices.

What exists is mainly:

- Vendor whitepapers (e.g., Zocdoc’s “What Should You Spend to Acquire a New Patient?”), which are **gated** and only described as containing “data from real practices” without exposing the underlying numbers publicly.[^6]
- Marketing agencies’ blog posts, which typically provide **example math**, not survey‑ or claims‑based ROI.

None of these meet the standard of “claims‑based or survey‑based benchmarks you can treat as industry numbers.”

***

## 3. How many patients per month/year a directory listing generates

There is **no large‑sample survey or claims analysis** that says, for example:

> “A typical Healthgrades listing generates 4 new patients per month,”
> or
> “Median Psychology Today profile yields 3 new clients/month.”

The closest you get are **small, anecdotal statements**:

- A review of therapist directories (Psypack) says, based on “discussions with practitioners,” that a Psychology Today profile can “fetch **1–5 referrals per month**” for many therapists. That’s a qualitative estimate, not a survey statistic.[^7]
- Wellnite reports that in a **2023 survey, ~53% of therapists said online directories were their primary source of new client referrals**.[^5]
    - This indicates **importance** of the channel but not **volume per profile**.

For PT/OT/SLP/ABA specifically, there is:

- **No quantified “patients per listing” data** in WebPT, APTA, CentralReach, or similar sources.

Any per‑listing patient‑volume numbers you see are either:

- Individual practices reporting their own experience, or
- Directory vendors citing internal metrics without methodology.

***

## 4. Cost‑per‑acquisition comparisons by channel (directories vs Google Ads vs social vs referrals)

Again, there is **no hard, therapy‑specific CAC comparison** that includes directory listings.

The best data that exist are **for other channels**:

### a) Google Ads – healthcare and PT benchmarks

From independent benchmark datasets:

- LocaliQ’s 2026 healthcare search benchmarks report for **Physical Therapy** search campaigns:
    - Average CPC ≈ **4.95 dollars**
    - Average cost per lead (CPL) ≈ **32.79 dollars**.[^8]
- Across all healthcare search campaigns:
    - Average CPC ≈ **5.64 dollars**
    - Average CPL ≈ **66.02 dollars**.[^8]

These are real, aggregated ad data, but they are **not directory‑specific**.

### b) Directory listing pricing → implied but not measured CPA

For directories like Psychology Today:

- Price: around **29.95 dollars/month** for a therapist listing.[^9]

If you *assume* (from anecdote) **1–5 new clients/month** per listing, then the **implied cost per acquired client** on listing fees alone is ~6–30 dollars/client. But:[^7]

- That “1–5 clients/month” is not a published industry benchmark, so the CPA here is **modeled**, not measured.
- Real CPA must factor in:
    - Your actual conversion rate from inquiries to booked patients,
    - Retention and revenue per case.

No large‑N study publishes **observed CPA from directories**.

### c) Social media and physician referrals

- There are **no published CAC benchmarks** for social media vs directories vs referrals in therapy specifically.
- General healthcare marketing content and Tebra/MGMA surveys show:
    - Most practices spend **1–5% of revenue on marketing** overall.[^10]
    - Physician and patient **referrals remain the dominant source of new patients** in many outpatient settings, with near‑zero marginal media cost, but their “CAC” is tied up in **relationship and quality investments**, not ad spend.

So with real data you can say:

- **Google Ads CPL** for PT ≈ 33 dollars/lead.[^8]
- **Directory listing fee** ≈ 30 dollars/month for Psych Today.[^9]

But you **cannot** claim rigorously that directories outperform or underperform Google Ads or referrals on CAC without inserting your own assumptions about:

- Leads/listing/month, and
- Lead‑to‑patient conversion.

***

## What this means for your LTV / volume modeling

Given the current evidence base:

- **What you *can* say with data**
    - Patients **heavily use online review and rating sites** when choosing providers (59–82% depending on survey).[^2][^1]
    - Many therapists report **directories as a primary source of referrals** (53% in one 2023 survey).[^5]
    - PT practices’ **Google Ads CPL** is in the **~33 dollars/lead** range, with healthcare‑wide CPL around 50–70 dollars/lead.[^8]
- **What you *cannot* say from published research**
    - Average number of patients/month generated per healthcare directory listing.
    - ROI/CAC comparisons between **directory listings vs Google Ads vs social vs physician referrals** for therapy practices.

To get the numbers you actually want (e.g., “a typical listing yields ~N new ASD therapy patients/year with CPA of X”), you would need either:

1. **Access to de‑identified directory platform data** (bookings per profile, by specialty and geography), or
2. A **multi‑practice survey** where clinics report:
    - New‑patient counts by source (per directory, per channel),
    - Their spend by channel.

Without that, any specific patient‑volume expectation for a directory listing will necessarily be **modeled**, not grounded in published, claims‑ or survey‑based data.
