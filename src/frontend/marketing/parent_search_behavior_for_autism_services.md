# What research exists on how parents of children with autism find and choose therapy providers? Specifically:

- What percentage of parents use online directories, Google search, physician referrals, or word of mouth to find therapists?
- How do parents evaluate and select therapy providers for their child with autism?
- What role do online reviews, directory listings, and provider profiles play in parent decision-making?
- Any surveys or studies on parent preferences when searching for autism services
- Data on how many providers a parent typically contacts or evaluates before choosing one

Prioritize peer-reviewed studies, university research, and surveys from organizations like Autism Speaks, ASHA, AOTA, or APTA. Not blog posts.

Research on how parents of autistic children find and choose therapy providers is surprisingly thin on hard percentages by channel (Google vs directories vs referrals). Most high‑quality work is qualitative or focuses on information needs and decision factors, not specific marketing channels. Below is what can be supported from peer‑reviewed studies and large surveys, with gaps called out.

***

## 1. How parents find out about therapies and providers

### Main information channels

Across multiple studies in different countries (Canada, Australia, New Zealand), parents consistently report using a *mix* of:

- Health and education professionals (pediatricians, psychologists, therapists, teachers)
- The internet (Google search, autism websites, forums, social media)
- Other parents and support groups
- Books, workshops, and research articles

Key sources:

1. **Shepherd et al., JADD 2018 – “Documenting and Understanding Parents’ Intervention Choices…” (New Zealand, n=570 parents)**
    - Cross‑sectional online survey about what interventions families use and what influences those choices.
    - Main findings (from abstract/summary):
        - **Funding/affordability** was a major determinant of which interventions were actually engaged.
        - **Medical professionals exerted the most influence** on parents’ intervention choices (more than other sources).[^1]
        - Sources of support (e.g., support groups) were *not* strongly predictive of which interventions were engaged, once other factors were controlled.[^1]
2. **Smith et al., BMC Complementary Medicine \& Therapies 2020 – qualitative study on CAM decision‑making (Australia, n=21 parents)**
    - Parents described being “lay researchers,” often starting from a point of little guidance after diagnosis.
    - **Almost all parents reported using the internet** to research treatments.[^2]
    - Other major information sources:
        - Books, magazines, research articles
        - Mainstream health professionals (GPs, psychologists, OTs, SLPs) – sometimes supportive, sometimes dismissive
        - **Other parents**, support groups, social media, online forums – highly trusted for “what really works”[^2]
3. **Altogether Autism / Shepherd et al. conference summary (New Zealand, ~500 parents)**
    - Online survey of parental choices across ABA, behavior therapy, SLT, OT, and diet.[^3]
    - About **80% of parents used more than one type of intervention**.[^3]
    - Information sources differed by modality:
        - SLT, OT, behavioral therapy: information mostly from **teachers, paediatricians, education staff**.
        - Diets: paediatricians + **internet**.
        - **ABA: largely from the internet and other parents.**[^3]
4. **National Needs Assessment Survey – Canada (CASDA, ~3,273 caregivers)**
    - Families reported using multiple services (mean 5.8 services for preschoolers; 7.8 for school‑age children; 8.9 for adults) over the child’s life, and identified a major unmet need as **“information on services”** and help navigating systems.[^4][^5]
    - The report focuses on service *use and gaps*, not exactly where parents heard about providers, but it underscores that **navigation and information access are top pain points**.[^5][^4]

### Do we know exact percentages by channel (Google vs directories vs referrals)?

No. None of the major peer‑reviewed or large‑scale surveys located report a clean breakdown such as:

> “X% of parents found their therapist via Google, Y% via physician referral, Z% via word‑of‑mouth.”

Instead, the quantitative work typically:

- Ranks **types of influencers** (e.g., medical professionals vs other parents vs funding) rather than marketing channels.[^1]
- Or simply notes that **internet use is nearly universal** among parents searching for information on treatments, often in combination with clinicians and other parents.[^2]

So any precise percentage split by “Google search vs directory vs physician referral” would be speculative.

***

## 2. How parents evaluate and select providers

Across studies, several consistent themes emerge for how parents evaluate therapies and providers for their autistic child:

### a) Perceived effectiveness and evidence

- In Shepherd et al. (NZ survey), **perceived effectiveness**, plus **funding/availability**, were key determinants of which interventions were actually used.[^1]
- In Smith et al. (CAM study), parents described wanting **“solid research”** (university studies, clinical trials) but also relied heavily on **other parents’ testimonials** when judging whether to try an approach.[^2]
    - Many parents accepted **multiple forms of evidence simultaneously**: scientific studies, clinician recommendations, and stories from other parents.[^2]


### b) Safety and “no harm” principle

- Parents emphasised avoiding harm; several explicitly said they would try an intervention **as long as it could not harm the child**, even if evidence was limited.[^2]
- CAM decisions were often pragmatic “trial‑and‑error” with careful observation, diaries, and willingness to discontinue if adverse effects occurred.[^2]


### c) Practical constraints: cost, funding, time, and feasibility

- Shepherd et al. found **funding was a major determinant of intervention engagement**.[^1]
- Smith et al. and Gentles et al. (2018 grounded‑theory study on navigating intervention) both highlight time, travel, scheduling, and family burden as central selection factors.[^6][^2]
    - Parents weighed whether they could realistically sustain the therapy alongside other family demands.


### d) Trust in professionals and other parents

- Shepherd et al.: **medical professionals were the most influential group** in parents’ choices overall.[^1]
- Smith et al.: mainstream healthcare professionals (GPs, psychologists, therapists) were **important but inconsistent**; some were supportive of non‑standard treatments, others dismissive.[^2]
- Parents frequently cited **other parents’ experiences as “valuable” or even decisive**—especially when multiple parents reported success with the same intervention.[^2]


### e) Fit with child and family

- Qualitative work (Gentles et al. 2018; Smith et al. 2020) shows parents look for:
    - Providers who **understand autism** and their child’s specific profile.
    - **Respectful communication and partnership**.
    - Approaches compatible with family values and daily routines.[^6][^2]

Overall, provider selection is **multi‑factorial**: parents balance evidence, safety, cost/funding, logistical feasibility, professional advice, and peer experiences.

***

## 3. Role of online reviews, directories, and provider profiles

Direct, quantitative evidence on *online reviews specifically* is very limited, but related findings:

### Internet use \& online information

- In Smith et al. (CAM decision‑making), **almost all parents used the internet extensively** to research treatments and providers; many described spending significant time on:
    - Google search
    - Online blogs and forums
    - Social media (especially Facebook groups)
    - Provider websites and practitioner information pages[^2]
- Parents reported **difficulty judging credibility** online and described cross‑checking:
    - Looking for physical addresses and credentials
    - Checking for consistency across multiple sources
    - Combining online information with direct testimonials from other parents[^2]


### Online directories and autism‑specific resource guides

- Autism Speaks’ **Resource Guide** is an online national database of autism providers and resources. Autism Speaks’ Autism Response Team (ART) explicitly directs families to use this directory to find local providers.[^7]
- The REACH Institute also advises clinicians to refer families to **Autism Speaks’ directory of resources by state**, particularly while they await or pursue diagnosis.[^8]

Neither Autism Speaks nor REACH publish usage percentages, but these tools are clearly treated as **key infrastructure** for families searching for services.

### Online testimonials and reviews

- In Smith et al., parents placed **strong weight on testimonials from other parents**, including those found online (blogs, forums, social media). Many described multiple parent success stories as more convincing than a sales rep or isolated claim.[^2]
- Parents used **reviews, testimonials, and informal online discussions** as a form of “evidence,” especially when formal research evidence was limited or confusing.[^2]

So while there is no large quantitative study on “how many parents rely on Google reviews or star ratings,” qualitative evidence shows:

- **Internet search and online communities are central** to information seeking.
- **Parent testimonials (including online) are highly influential** in decision‑making.
- Families want a **central, trustworthy online source** that aggregates reliable information about interventions and providers.[^2]

## 4. Surveys and studies on parent preferences when searching for autism services

The main research streams that speak to parent preferences:

### a) Intervention choice \& influencers

- **Shepherd et al., JADD 2018 (New Zealand, n=570)** – “Documenting and Understanding Parents’ Intervention Choices…”[^1]
    - Quantitatively examined:
        - Which interventions were being used.
        - Which were rejected or not tried.
        - Factors associated with engagement (funding, symptom severity, etc.).
    - Key conclusions:
        - **Funding/affordability** was a major determinant of using an intervention.
        - **Medical professionals** were the primary influencing group for many decisions.
        - Parents’ perception of the child’s symptom severity also impacted choices.[^1]


### b) Information‑seeking and decision‑making models

- **Smith et al. 2020 (Australia, n=21)** – CAM information‑seeking and decision‑making.[^2]
    - Parents’ preferences:
        - Combine **scientific evidence** (journals, university sources) with **other parents’ experiences**.
        - Have access to a **central, authoritative, online resource** summarizing evidence and risks.
        - Avoid harmful treatments while remaining open to pragmatic trial‑and‑error.
- **Gentles et al. 2018 (Canada) – “Parent engagement in autism-related care: a grounded theory study”**[^6]
    - Developed a model of how parents **navigate intervention across development**, including:
        - Recognizing concerns → seeking information → engaging with services → re‑evaluating and reconfiguring the “portfolio” of interventions.
    - Shows that parents’ preferences evolve as they gain experience; they repeatedly re‑assess providers and interventions over time.
- **Factors related to treatment choice – review**
    - A review article in *Research in Autism Spectrum Disorders* (“Factors related to parents’ choices of treatments for their children with ASD”, summarised in ScienceDirect abstract) synthesizes earlier work (Green et al. 2006; Goin‑Kochel et al. 2007; etc.) and concludes that:[^9]
        - Choice is shaped by **perceived child severity**, **perceived effectiveness** and **side effects**, **family values**, and **recommendations from professionals and other parents**.
        - Many families use **multiple interventions concurrently**, and often change their therapy mix over time.


### c) Service needs \& navigation preferences

- **Hodgetts et al. 2015 – “Profile and predictors of service needs for families of children with ASD” (Canada, n=143)**[^5]
    - Top overall and unmet needs: **information on services, family support, and respite care**.
    - Families generally reported that **funding and quality of professional support** were viewed positively, but **navigation and information access remained problematic**.[^5]
- **CASDA National Needs Assessment (Canada, ~3,273 caregivers)**[^4]
    - Echoed that *information on services* and *system navigation* were major unmet needs.
    - Families clearly want **centralized, easy‑to‑understand information** about available services across sectors (health, education, community).[^4][^5]

These studies do not report “search UX preferences” in the sense of interface design, but together they indicate parents want:

- Trustworthy, **evidence‑based information in one place**.
- Clear indication of which interventions are **evidence‑based vs fringe**.
- **Guidance from clinicians** paired with **real‑world experiences from other parents**.
- Help navigating multiple systems and funding sources.

***

## 5. How many providers do parents contact or evaluate before choosing one?

Here the empirical base is weakest. No study was found that directly asks:

> “How many therapists or agencies did you contact or interview before choosing your current provider?”

However, there are some related data points:

- CASDA national survey: across the lifespan, children used on average:
    - **5.8 different services** (preschoolers)
    - **7.8 services** (school‑age children)
    - **8.9 services** (adults), as reported by caregivers.[^4]
    - This reflects the **number of services accessed**, not the number of providers contacted and rejected, but it shows that families typically engage with **many services over time**, not just a single provider.
- Altogether Autism / Shepherd conference summary: ~80% of parents had sought interventions from **more than one type of intervention** (SLT, OT, ABA, dietary, behavior therapy).[^3]
- Qualitative studies (Smith 2020; Gentles 2018) describe parents **cycling through multiple providers and therapies**, trialing and discontinuing some, but do not quantify how many providers they contacted before committing.[^6][^2]

So the best that can be said from real data is:

- Families often engage with **multiple services and interventions across childhood**, and many trial and discontinue some approaches.
- There is **no robust quantitative measure** of “average number of providers contacted before choosing one” for autism services in the peer‑reviewed literature.

***

## Summary: What is *known* vs *unknown*

**Well‑supported by peer‑reviewed or large‑scale data**

- Parents of autistic children typically rely on a combination of:
    - **Medical/educational professionals** (high influence; especially for initial choices).[^1]
    - The **internet** (almost universal; especially for CAM and ABA).[^3][^2]
    - **Other parents/support groups** (testimonials highly valued, sometimes more than formal marketing).[^3][^2]
- Major decision factors for choosing therapies/providers:
    - **Funding/cost and availability**.[^1]
    - Perceived **effectiveness**, **safety**, and **fit** for the child and family.[^9][^2]
    - **Severity of the child’s symptoms**.[^9][^1]
    - Trust/rapport with clinicians and parent networks.[^6][^2]
- Parents strongly desire:
    - **Central, trustworthy online resources** summarizing evidence and listing services.[^4][^2]
    - Support for **system navigation and information on services**, which is consistently reported as a major unmet need.[^5][^4]

**Not available in current research**

- Percentages of parents using:
    - Google vs online directories vs social media vs insurance directories vs physician referrals.
- Quantitative impact metrics of:
    - Online reviews or star ratings on actual provider choice.
- Average number of **providers contacted** before choosing one.

For anything at that level of granularity, you would have to run an original survey (e.g., of families in a given region) or obtain data from a large autism‑specific directory platform and analyze user behavior.

If you want, the next step could be to outline a short survey instrument that would capture the exact metrics you care about (channels used, number of providers contacted, role of reviews) in a way that is consistent with the literature and could be fielded via parent groups, clinics, or autism organizations.
