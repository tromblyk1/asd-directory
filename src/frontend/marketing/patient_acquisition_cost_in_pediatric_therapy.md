# What is the average patient acquisition cost (CAC) for pediatric therapy clinics (PT, OT, speech therapy, ABA) in the United States? I'm looking for:

- Average cost per new patient lead from Google Ads for therapy-related keywords
- Average cost per new patient acquisition for pediatric therapy practices
- Conversion rates from lead to patient in therapy settings
- How directory listings and referral sources compare to paid advertising for patient acquisition cost
- Any published data on marketing spend as a percentage of revenue for therapy practices

I want real data from industry sources, practice management surveys, or marketing benchmarks — not blog estimates. Sources like WebPT surveys, APTA practice surveys, or healthcare marketing studies preferred.

There are no publicly available benchmarks that give a clean, therapy‑specific CAC number for **pediatric** PT/OT/SLP/ABA in the U.S. All of the rigorous, aggregated data that exist are:

- Specialty‑level **Google Ads benchmarks** (often “physical therapy” rather than pediatric‑only).
- Cross‑healthcare benchmarks for **cost per lead (CPL)** and **patient acquisition cost (PAC)**.
- General healthcare surveys on **marketing spend as a % of revenue**.
- Isolated case studies with explicit numbers.

Below is what can be supported with real data, with pediatric‑/ABA‑specific gaps called out.

***

## 1. Cost per lead from Google Ads for therapy‑related keywords

### a) Physical therapy–specific Google Ads benchmarks

LocaliQ’s 2026 “Healthcare Search Ads Benchmarks” analyzed thousands of U.S. healthcare search campaigns and broke out results for 16 specialties, including **Physical Therapy**.[^1]

Key metrics for **physical therapy search ads**:

- **Average CPC (cost per click):** 4.95 dollars[^1]
- **Average cost per lead (CPL):** 32.79 dollars[^1]

Industry‑wide across healthcare search ads in the same dataset:

- **Overall healthcare CPC:** 5.64 dollars
- **Overall healthcare conversion rate (click → lead):** 8.09%
- **Overall healthcare CPL:** 66.02 dollars[^1]

Interpretation for your use case:

- For PT‑related keywords, **Google Ads leads are averaging about 33 dollars per lead** in this dataset, substantially lower than the healthcare‑wide average of 66 dollars.[^1]
- This is **not pediatric‑only** and not OT/SLP/ABA‑specific; it is dominated by adult ortho PT, but it is the clearest empirical benchmark for rehab therapy keywords.


### b) Cross‑healthcare CPC/CPL benchmarks

Several large benchmark datasets give broader ranges that set expectations for therapy‑adjacent campaigns:

- **WordStream healthcare benchmark (search, 2021)** for “Hospitals \& Clinics”:
    - Average cost per lead: **33.45 dollars**
    - Average CPC: 3.63 dollars[^2]
- **WordStream healthcare benchmark (social, 2021)** shows social ads generally have **much lower CPCs** but higher CPLs and lower intent; still, hospitals/clinics CPLs remained under about 50 dollars.[^2]
- **Scientissimum (2025) healthcare paid search snapshot**, based on aggregated account data:
    - Healthcare Google Ads **CPC:** about **4.22 dollars**
    - Healthcare search ads **conversion rate:** about **2.6%**
    - Healthcare **CPL:** about **53.53 dollars**, with **Hospitals \& Clinics** at **32.14 dollars** and cosmetic surgery at 134.29 dollars per lead.[^3]

Taken together:

- For **clinic‑type healthcare providers**, cost per lead from Google search ads tends to cluster in the **30–70 dollars/lead** range, with **physical therapy at the low end (~33 dollars/lead)**.[^3][^2][^1]

There are **no equivalent benchmark tables** for “pediatric OT”, “pediatric speech therapy”, or “ABA therapy” as separate Google Ads verticals. Those tend to be grouped into “hospitals \& clinics,” “health services,” or “behavioral/mental health,” where CPLs are often higher (mental health CPLs > 100 dollars in some datasets).[^1]

## 2. Average cost per new patient acquisition (not just lead) for therapy practices

Here the data are much thinner, and nothing is pediatric‑therapy‑specific.

### a) Cross‑healthcare patient acquisition cost

A healthcare digital marketing analysis (Direction.com, 2025) citing **Healthcare Finance News (2024)** reports:[^4]

- **Average patient acquisition cost in healthcare: 150–300 dollars per new patient** (all specialties, all channels).[^4]

This is **per new patient, not per lead**, and includes costs across marketing channels and operational friction.

### b) Implied CAC if you combine PT CPL and generic conversion benchmarks

No dataset directly reports “average CAC per new PT patient” at national scale. However, you can combine:

- LocaliQ’s **PT CPL ~32.79 dollars** from Google search ads[^1]
- Generic healthcare **lead‑to‑appointment conversion** norms:

A growth‑metrics guide for medical practices (cross‑specialty) suggests a reasonable **lead‑to‑appointment conversion benchmark of about 60%**, with phone inquiries often ~70% and web forms ~40%.[^5]

If a PT clinic:

- Pays ~33 dollars per Google Ads lead, and
- Converts ~60% of those inquiries into scheduled new‑patient visits,

then the **implied CAC from Google search** is on the order of:

- 33 dollars ÷ 0.60 ≈ **55 dollars per new patient** (for that channel, ignoring no‑shows).

That is **derived**, not directly reported. The only hard numbers in that chain are:

- PT CPL ≈ 32.79 dollars from LocaliQ[^1]
- A general healthcare benchmark that **sub‑50% lead‑to‑appointment is poor**, and ~60% is reasonable.[^5]

By contrast, the cross‑healthcare 150–300 dollars/patient figure from Healthcare Finance News (as cited) includes **all channels** and specialties, including very expensive ones (addiction, plastic surgery, mental health).[^4]

There are **no published CAC benchmarks** that:

- Focus only on **rehab therapy**,
- Distinguish pediatric from adult, or
- Include ABA separately.

Any therapy‑specific CAC number in the wild is almost always a **practice’s own KPI**, not an industry‑wide survey statistic.

## 3. Conversion rates from lead to patient in therapy / healthcare

There are no big, therapy‑only conversion surveys, but there are a few relevant data points:

### a) Cross‑healthcare lead‑to‑appointment conversion

The Rework “Healthcare Practice Metrics” guide (covering medical practices broadly) defines the **lead‑to‑appointment conversion rate** and uses this example:[^5]

- If you receive 100 new patient inquiries and schedule 60 appointments, your conversion rate is **60%**, and **rates below 50%** are flagged as problematic.

They also note:

- Phone inquiries typically convert **higher** (around 70%)
- Website form submissions often convert **lower** (around 40%).[^5]

This is not rehab‑specific, but it is the most explicit benchmark‑style statement on inquiry→appointment conversion.

### b) Rehab therapy example: WebPT Reach case

A WebPT marketing case quote (for a multi‑clinic rehab provider using WebPT Reach) reports:

- “Leads for appointment requests increased by 20%, and **conversion of these leads has been close to 90%**.”[^6]

This is a single implementation, not a benchmark, but it shows that **high‑performing rehab funnels can convert 80–90% of marketing leads into scheduled patients** when front‑desk workflows are optimized.

### c) Show rates / no‑shows

For outpatient rehab generally (not marketing‑specific), Net Health and others call out:

- Typical **no‑show + late cancellation** rates around **10–12%**; above 15–20% significantly harms revenue.[^7]

So if:

- 60–70% of leads become scheduled appointments, and
- ~85–90% of scheduled new patients actually show,

then a **lead‑to-seen‑patient** conversion in the **50–60% range** is a reasonable high‑level benchmark in well‑run practices. Again, that is constructed from separate benchmarks, not from a single therapy‑only dataset.

## 4. Directory listings \& referrals vs paid advertising on CAC

There is almost no hard dollar CAC comparison across channels for therapy‑specific practices, but there are solid directional data:

### a) Source mix: referrals vs digital

A SpryPT article summarizing research on physical therapy marketing notes that:

- Well‑designed **referral programs account for 35–65% of a practice’s referral sources**, i.e., a large share of new patients still come from **physician referrals and word‑of‑mouth**, not paid media.[^8]

That aligns with common findings in PT practice management: physician and patient referrals remain the **dominant source of new patients**, with digital (SEO, Google Business Profile, site, reviews) and PPC providing incremental volume.

### b) Effective spend on directories / SEO vs paid ads

A 2025 Tebra survey of private medical practices (cross‑specialty, but heavy on outpatient clinics) found:[^9]

- **62% of practices spend 1–5% of revenue on marketing**,
- 14% spend 6–10%,
- 14% spend 11–40%.[^9]

For specific digital line items annually (all practices):[^9]

- SEO:
    - 33% spend 1–999 dollars/year;
    - 40% spend **0 dollars** on SEO.
- Social ads:
    - 43% spend 100–990 dollars/year;
    - 25% spend 1,000–2,499;
    - 32% spend > 2,500.
- Print/billboards: similar modest figures.[^9]

For many small clinics, that implies:

- **Very low absolute spend** on SEO and social/directory presence, but
- Meaningful new patient flow from **Google Business Profile, organic search, and insurer directories** simply because these channels piggyback off fixed overhead (website, EMR, reputation) rather than high marginal ad spend.

By contrast, Google Ads and other PPC channels generate **explicit CPLs** in the 30–70 dollars range for healthcare, as above.[^3][^2][^1]

So on a cost‑per‑new‑patient basis:

- **Physician \& patient referrals**: marginal CAC is near **0 dollars in media spend**, but requires non‑trivial investment in **relationships, outcomes, and internal processes**.
- **Directories / local SEO / reputation**: low ongoing cash outlay (often a few thousand dollars per year total), but can drive a large share of organic and “free” inquiries—so **very low CAC** once fixed costs are sunk.[^9]
- **Paid search (Google Ads)**: **CPL ~33 dollars for PT** and ~66 dollars for healthcare overall; after lead‑to‑patient conversion, CAC may land in the **50–150 dollars/patient** band for many specialties, lower for PT.[^4][^5][^1]

There is **no published therapy‑only CAC comparison by channel**, but all available evidence suggests:

- **Referrals + directories/SEO** are **far cheaper per patient** than PPC once running well,
- Paid search is the **most controllable, scalable lever** for added volume, at a **known marginal CAC**.

## 5. Marketing spend as a percentage of revenue (therapy \& healthcare)

No one has published a **WebPT‑ or APTA‑style “PT marketing spend as % of revenue” benchmark**. The best available data are:

### a) Cross‑practice (all specialties) survey – Tebra 2025

Tebra surveyed U.S. private practices regarding marketing budgets and found:[^9]

- **10% of practices** spend **0%** of revenue on marketing.
- **62%** spend **1–5% of revenue** on marketing.
- **14%** spend **6–10%** of revenue.
- **14%** spend **11–40%** of revenue.[^9]

This includes primary care, specialty clinics, dental, etc., but outpatient rehab clinics are part of this mix.

### b) MGMA perspective on overhead

MGMA’s commentary on overhead for outpatient medical groups notes that:[^10]

- Labor typically consumes **50–60%+ of revenue**.
- Technology and IT expenses are about **2–3% of revenue**.
- Facility costs (rent, utilities, maintenance) add **5–10%**.
- **“Other overhead”**, including **marketing and advertising**, staff training, and equipment depreciation, collectively adds “a few percentage points” of revenue.[^10]

This is consistent with Tebra’s finding that most practices cluster in the **1–5% of revenue** marketing band.[^9]

### c) PT‑specific recommendations (not survey‑based)

PT‑specific consultants and marketing agencies (e.g., Practice Promotions, Paul Gough, Breakthrough) frequently **recommend**:

- Spending **5% of revenue** to maintain volume and
- **7–10% (or more)** for growth, framed as adaptation of SBA small‑business guidelines.[^11][^12][^13]

However, those are **expert recommendations**, not aggregated spending data. For “real data,” the **Tebra + MGMA** figures above are stronger: they show that, in practice, **most outpatient clinics (including PT) actually sit around 1–5% of revenue for marketing**, with a minority going higher.[^10][^9]

There is **no WebPT or APTA practice profile** that breaks out marketing as a line‑item % of revenue.

## 6. ABA and pediatric‑specific gaps

For your specific interest in **pediatric therapy and ABA**:

- None of the major marketing benchmark studies (LocaliQ, WordStream, Scientissimum, Tebra, MGMA) provide **pediatric‑only** or **ABA‑only** breakouts.
- ABA programs are often coded under **behavioral health / mental health / addiction recovery** in ad platforms, where CPLs can be **much higher** than for PT (mental health CPL around 141 dollars in LocaliQ’s data). That gives a directional sense that **ABA‑focused paid search could easily see CPLs > 100 dollars** if targeted like other behavioral specialties, but there is no large ABA‑only benchmark.[^1]
- No published WebPT, CentralReach, or practice‑management survey has publicly reported **marketing CAC, CPL, or marketing spend % of revenue** for pediatric PT/OT/SLP/ABA specifically.

Any numeric CAC you see marketed as “average ABA patient acquisition cost” is almost certainly:

- Derived from a single agency’s client set,
- Not independently benchmarked across the industry, and
- Not separated by pediatric vs adult.

***

## How to apply this to your modeling

If you want to build **defensible CAC / LTV models** for pediatric therapy and ABA with real data:

1. **Anchor your paid search lead costs** on the best available benchmarks:
    - PT search CPL: **≈ 33 dollars/lead**.[^1]
    - Healthcare‑wide search CPL: **≈ 53–66 dollars/lead**.[^3][^1]
2. **Use cross‑healthcare lead‑to‑appointment norms** to bracket conversion:
    - 50–70% inquiry→appointment from Rework’s guidance and typical call vs web form behavior.[^5]
3. **Back into CAC per patient** for each channel explicitly, labeling it as **modeled from benchmarks**, not observed.
4. **Treat referrals + directories as extremely low marginal CAC** channels (effectively “near zero” in media spend) and calibrate their volume contribution using your own data (source‑of‑new‑patient fields).
5. For **marketing spend % of revenue**, assume:
    - Most clinics function at **1–5% of revenue** today,[^9]
    - 5–10% represents a growth‑oriented posture (based on small‑business/consultant guidance).

Given the current state of the evidence, there is **no single authoritative CAC number for pediatric therapy clinics**. The best you can do is:

- Use healthcare and PT‑specific Google Ads benchmarks for **CPL**,
- Combine with **generic healthcare conversion benchmarks** for CAC ranges, and
- Ground your high‑value channels (referrals, directories, payer lists) in **your own practice data**, recognizing their CAC is much lower than paid media.
