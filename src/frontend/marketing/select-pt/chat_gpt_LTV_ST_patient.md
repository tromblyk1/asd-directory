Now we’re getting into the interesting one.

Speech therapy (SLP) has a totally different LTV profile depending on **population**. Adult outpatient speech is usually short. Pediatric speech can be long. Really long.

Let’s separate it cleanly.

Adult outpatient speech (post-stroke, TBI, voice disorders, swallowing rehab):

Typical episode:
• 6–12 visits
• Reimbursement roughly $90–$160 per visit

Math:

6 × $100 = $600
10 × $120 = $1,200
12 × $150 = $1,800

So adult outpatient speech usually lands around **$800–$1,500 per episode**, very similar to PT and OT on the short-duration end.

Now pediatric speech is a completely different beast.

Speech delay, articulation disorders, language disorders, autism-related communication issues — these are often:

• 1–2 visits per week
• 6 months to multiple years
• $90–$170 per visit depending on payer

Let’s run conservative math.

1 visit/week × 40 weeks × $120
= **$4,800 per year**

2 visits/week × 40 weeks × $120
= **$9,600 per year**

Now extend that 2–3 years (which is not uncommon):

$5k–$10k per year × 2–3 years
= **$10k–$30k lifetime revenue per child**

And some kids stay in speech from preschool through elementary school. That can quietly stack.

Early intervention (0–3 years) is often publicly funded, so reimbursement may be lower. Private insurance pediatric clinics tend to be higher revenue per visit.

School-based SLP is salaried, so that’s not revenue-per-patient in a business sense.

So here’s the blunt summary:

Adult outpatient SLP:
≈ **$800–$1,500 per episode**

Pediatric outpatient SLP:
≈ **$4k–$10k per year**

Multi-year pediatric cases:
≈ **$10k–$30k+ lifetime**

Speech tends to have higher duration than PT, similar or sometimes longer than OT in pediatrics, and often better retention because communication development doesn’t “heal” in 8 visits.

Now here’s the real lever if you’re thinking entrepreneurially:

Speech has:
• High demand
• Waitlists everywhere
• Strong parent motivation
• Often lower equipment overhead than PT

But it also has:
• Insurance authorization chaos
• Staffing shortages
• Burnout risk

Healthcare economics always come back to systems. The money is in operational efficiency and retention — not billing one extra unit.

Zoom out even further and something fascinating appears: pediatric therapy businesses aren’t episodic care models. They’re semi-longitudinal developmental partners. That changes the entire LTV strategy compared to adult rehab.

And that’s where the strategic advantage hides.
