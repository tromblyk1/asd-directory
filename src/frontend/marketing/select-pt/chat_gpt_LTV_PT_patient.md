Now we’re talking business metrics. I like it.

“Lifetime Value” (LTV) just means: **How much total revenue does one patient generate over the full course of care?** Not profit. Revenue. Big difference.

For a typical outpatient physical therapy patient in the U.S., here’s the practical reality:

Most patients attend **8–12 visits** per episode of care.
Average reimbursement per visit (blended insurance + Medicare + occasional cash pay) runs roughly **$90–$150 per visit**, depending on payer mix and geography.

So the math:

* Low end: 8 visits × $90 = **~$720**
* Mid range: 10 visits × $120 = **~$1,200**
* High end: 12 visits × $150 = **~$1,800**

That puts the **typical LTV per episode around $900–$1,500** for most standard outpatient ortho cases.

Now here’s where it gets interesting.

That’s per episode, not necessarily per lifetime human. Some patients return for:

* A second injury
* Post-surgical rehab
* Chronic flare-ups
* Maintenance programs

If someone comes back twice over 3–5 years, you could realistically see **$2,000–$3,000 total lifetime revenue** from one individual.

But — and this matters — PT is usually episodic, not subscription-based. Most clinics do not have recurring revenue unless they build:

* Cash-based maintenance memberships
* Performance training add-ons
* Wellness programs
* Direct access marketing funnels

Clinics that do that well can push LTV north of **$3k–$5k per patient over time**.

Now let’s be blunt: if you’re thinking entrepreneurially (which you usually are), the key lever isn’t squeezing more visits out of one injury. It’s:

* Increasing completion rates (patients drop off early all the time)
* Improving payer mix
* Adding ancillary services
* Reducing cancellations
* Lowering acquisition cost

If your acquisition cost is $200–$300 per patient and LTV is $1,200, that’s a very workable business.

Bottom Line:
Most outpatient PT patients generate roughly **$1,000–$1,500 per episode**, and maybe **$2k–$3k lifetime** unless the clinic builds recurring services.

Healthcare economics are weird. It’s not about one big sale — it’s about volume, retention, and payer contracts. That’s where the real leverage hides.
