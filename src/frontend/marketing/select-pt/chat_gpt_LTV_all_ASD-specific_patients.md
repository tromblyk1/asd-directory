Now that’s the right question.

You’re not asking “what’s LTV?”
You’re asking: **does diagnosis materially change LTV inside pediatrics?**

Short answer: yes. Often dramatically.

But let’s think it through methodically instead of hand-waving.

First: what drives LTV in pediatric therapy?

It’s basically:

Visit frequency × duration (in years) × reimbursement rate × retention

So the only way autism changes LTV is if it changes one of those variables.

And it does.

Let’s break it down cleanly.

---

## Typical Pediatric (Non-Autism) Case

Common examples:

* Articulation disorder
* Mild language delay
* Fine motor delay
* Post-op ortho
* Feeding issue

These often look like:

1 session/week
3–9 months
Sometimes 6–12 months max

So math:

1× weekly × 40 weeks × $110–$140
≈ **$4k–$6k for a year**

Many discharge sooner. Some wrap at 6 months. So real-world average often lands:

**$2k–$6k total episode value**

Duration is the limiter.

---

## Pediatric Autism Case

Autism changes two key variables:

1. Frequency
2. Duration

Autism-related therapy often looks like:

1–3 sessions/week per discipline
Multi-year duration
High retention
High parent motivation

And sometimes co-treatment across PT + OT + ST.

Let’s do conservative math for just speech:

2× weekly × 45 weeks × $120
≈ $10,800 per year

Now stretch that across 3 years:

≈ $32k

And that’s one discipline.

Add OT at similar frequency:

You can realistically see **$20k–$60k+ total lifetime revenue per child** across multiple services over several years.

Not because anyone is exploiting anything.
Because autism is developmental and chronic, not episodic.

That’s the structural difference.

---

## Why Autism Cases Retain Longer

* Developmental trajectory is ongoing
* Gains are incremental
* Plateau periods still justify maintenance
* Parents are highly engaged
* Schools often reinforce therapy need

Compare that to articulation-only kids, who often graduate in under a year.

---

## The Catch

Autism cases also bring:

* Higher documentation burden
* Insurance auth churn
* More denied claims
* Higher staff burnout
* Behavioral complexity
* Longer evaluation time

So LTV is higher — but operational cost is also higher.

The margin is what matters, not just revenue.

---

## How You’d Actually Verify This

If you wanted real data instead of theory:

1. Look at CPT utilization patterns in commercial payer datasets.
2. Compare average authorization duration by ICD-10 (e.g., F84.x autism spectrum disorder vs F80.x speech delay).
3. Pull state Medicaid therapy utilization reports.
4. Analyze claims frequency in FAIR Health or CMS datasets.
5. Survey large pediatric therapy chains’ annual reports (if available).

You’d want:
Mean visits per patient
Mean months in care
Per-discipline utilization

That would give you a true actuarial LTV comparison.

---

## Bottom Line

Typical pediatric (non-autism):
≈ **$2k–$6k per episode**

Pediatric autism:
Often **$10k–$40k+ over multiple years**, depending on services

Autism changes the time horizon. And in business, time horizon is everything.

Healthcare economics get weird when chronic developmental conditions enter the picture. They shift a clinic from episodic repair to longitudinal partnership.

That’s not just a billing difference.
That’s a completely different business model hiding under the same CPT codes.

And that’s where strategic insight lives.
